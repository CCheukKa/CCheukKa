# 2021

## September

### 26-09-2021
#### Farewell Toby
> This is the very first entry of this journal thing but let's not dwell on this seemingly monumental topic to make way for a much more significant event.
> 
> It was Toby's last day in Hong Kong. He is going to the University of Oxford to study Mathematics or something. We've already had a farewell meal on 24/09 but that's besides the point. CNok, Efren, Samuel, and myself went to the airport to give him a final farewell. The airport was quite non-crowded due to COVID or something. I have never a place in Hong Kong this non-crowded before so that was quite amusing, not to mention it was my first time in 3 years I've been to the airport. He was queuing for the check-in at the British Airways counters when Teddy and Adrian Lee/Li somehow spontaneously materialised. We had a brief meal at the airport - nothing too remarkable except it being horribly expensive, but I guess it's to be expected. His dad did give all of us red packets after the meal as a thank-you for us coming along to the airport. I think he had like 6 or something relatives with him: parents + granny + some other randos. We had some photos and hugs. His dad and granny left just after one photo which struck me as odd but that's besides the point. Come to think of it, I've never hug him before I don't think, nor have I been in a group hug before, so that was interesting and awkward, especially considering we had to hold it long enough for his relatives to take pictures of this mess of arms and torsos. Alas, it was time for him to enter the security gate thing, despite it being a whopping 1.5 hours too early, and there shall no longer be a turning back. He struggled a bit with the first self-help gate thing, which I kindly helped myself to documenting in the form of a video, later IG story. We waved goodbye intensely but he barely looked back. We kept watching until we lost sight of him. CNok stated he wanted to cry a bit but ultimately didn't. But he did say that if I were the one to leave, then he would definitely cry rolling on the floor.
> 
> I wasn't too emotional. It might kicked in later after it had sunk on me. Though it did made me have a sudden realisation. Life is *not* constant, nothing is. I have been taking it for granted that life is always going to be "unchanging" and everything should have a "later". But it isn't and doesn't. It just made me question my mortality for a second, even after a day, as the time of writing this, and I'd expect this feeling to grow as time goes on, every time I'd talk to him, every time we'd have some sort of now Toby-less gathering, and as I get older, in general. For instance, Toby and I have been planning for this dumb devil comedy sketch idea since before DSE even. We had planned for something to happen during the summer holidays. We have much planned for the summer actually: variety chess videos, podcasts (which honestly might still be possible), some local hotel staycations, vacation trips to the UK even. With much ambition, might I add. Guess what? Nothing got done. Like literally not a single thing got done in that summer. It was literally the longest summer I have ever had in my entire life and I did nothing. None of us did. Why? 'Cus of that mindset that life is constant, and that it would always have a "later". It *is* a huge let-down and regret for me, and more importantly, for Toby. It should be a whole year soonest until we meet again. But I guess at least I got to walk around CUHK with Toby on 17-Sep, so that's something. Also us the gang had been holding more gatherings and events in S6, probably because it was the last year of our secondary school years. I have CNok to thank for that, for not letting the mindset get to us, especially me, at least for a short while.
>
> I wish it doesn't take such a huge thing for me to realise that but the sad truth is it did. I would say this would be a life lesson for me, and that I would change my ways after this, but the truth is, there had been so many life lessons before this, and I can't say how many of them have really changed me - nay - budged me even the tiniest bit. Maybe they have, maybe they haven't, I don't know. Everything is subconscious after all. They may have been memorable, sure, no less than this one, but they may ultimately prove themselves meaningless if I couldn't really get something out of them. Ambition is a pretty significant source of regrets, but the lack of it accounts for the large majority. I guess I am grateful that those plans existed while they existed. At least we got to discuss about it despite we both knowing that they probably won't happen in time. I shall miss him.
> 
> I have known him since S1. Weirdly enough, I actually think he might be my first new friend in SJC, along with Bennie. We did dumb stuff back then like stalking Santos, but ultimately we just have the same channel, at least I think we do. We fell out during S2-3 due to reasons still beyond my comprehension till this day, but we got back together by chance(?) from S4 onwards. Speaking of S1 and Bennie, the three of us actually did try to make our own game, along with Marwin I believe. Well it was mainly *my* game, but they contributed in the form of 3D models. Toby also contributed some ideas(?) I believe, though the memories are starting to get vague and foggy. It never got finished, partly because my computer sucked back then, partly because of that "life is constant" mindset. That "later" turned into "never". It goes to show our ambition even back then. Guess we haven't changed too much. He was a very plastic person back then. And honestly, I would go as far as to say that he *was* the advocate for my own plasticity and that he was the major reason my personality and mode of thinking when it comes to dealing with new people and stuff are the way they are. Yes, our ambition never changed, but neither did my/our procrastinating mentality. But I guess it was all me. Toby did try to push the ideas into fruition but I was somewhat reluctant in some of them for reasons. I shall take fault for that.
> 
> Anyway, us the gang planned to visit the UK and have some sort of vacation there, with Toby of course, some time in the future, presumably after the fucking COVID-19 ends. Let's see what would be the fate of this plan: shall it fall like so many before it have, or would it be the one that shall finally rise above all? Either way, it shall be at least a year until we can find out.
> 
> Hope Toby won't get too lonely in the UK. He never struck me as being too emotionally attached too much to anything anyway, so I guess he would most likely be fine, probably. Though he was a really messy person when I first met him, and still is. Let's hope the two luggages of stuff shall only allow him to make the smallest mess at worst. Whatever awaits, shall it be bright and vibrant. Farewell Toby, until we meet again.

### 28-09-2021
#### Getting back my HKDSE papers
> I bought the HKDSE papers some time ago, all the subjects, all the papers, and I got them back today from the post office. It's interesting 'cus it has already been so long ago that if not for the SMS they sent me, I would've completely forgotten about this. I remember being super bummed when the DSE results were out, and rightfully so. I was in some sort of stages of grief. My entire DSE and JUPAS journey was something of a roller coaster really. It started on the day when the results were out. Obviously I have made score predictions and some plans depending on the actual final score, but it's safe to say that all of those went completely out of the window - no joke. Even in my worst planned scenarios, I should still be able to get into at least A2, and hopefully transfer back to A1; but the actual results turned out worse than the worst, far from it even. I was in shock, very much so. To be fair, it was to be expected that the scores wouldn't be the best, but being worse than the worst scenario was never expected. I guess all of my friends got unsatisfactory results too, as did the whole school. I guess Toby got the best out of this whole ordeal, but I guess this wasn't much of a doubt in the first place. He was never bad at anything really. His academics mainly just consisted of his qualities with no setbacks like so many of us do. I was planning to get at least a 5** in Maths and/or Physics, and some 5* elsewhere. I guess maybe I was being too optimistic, but to be honest I didn't think I failed this badly when I made those score predictions, right after finishing the papers. Some of the subjects were a little wonky, all things considered, especially for Biology where I was sick the whole time and spent something like 15 minutes in the washroom. CNok was aiming to get 5** in both Chinese and Economics. He had been doing a lot of Economics past papers, like *a lot* a lot. But ultimately he still was able to got into a rather decent programme. I mean universities do offer a huge variety of business and economics programmes, with a huge range of score requirements, which translates to students being able to apply for suitable programmes no matter what score they ended up achieving. Not me though, which brings me back to the main point of discussion.
> 
> Upon receiving the results, I immediately applied for a remarking the very same day. It was for English, Liberal Studies, Chemistry, and Biology I believe. Fortunately for me, I was able to successfully upgrade from 5 to 5* for my Biology, which was somewhat surprising considering it was my least confident subject. But either way, this gave me a ticket for a JUPAS reconsideration, which by some tricks of the system, would actually make it easier for me to get into the programmes I wanted. From the statistics database my school had provided, my score actually reached the median of last year's admission round of my A2 programme. I was really confident and under the impression that this would be it. It wasn't. The reconsideration yielded no result and it was ultimately futile. It wasn't common to succeed in DSE appeals, you know. Biology, even less. I was told that I was the first person in SJC's history who had ever had success in appealing Biology. It was pretty disappointing. I was teased but it was snatched away from my in the very last moment. I was really more surprised that I didn't got accepted into the programme despite the data suggesting otherwise. It could only imply that the cut-off was exceptionally high this year. I guess those born in 2003 was just fated to be eternally unlucky. SARS was a thing when we were born, some sort of swine flu in 2009 took my kindergarten graduation ceremony, the principal got changed during my S6 year, and the whole COVID-19 ordeal of course. None of this makes any scientific sense but in a way I guess I have already adopted the belief that bad luck was one of my personal traits. Basically every big event in my life went haywire. I have made nothing short of 4 preference priority lists within the past month or so, and I don't think any one of them went my way, literally, for one reason or another, mostly for reasons beyond my control.
>
> So anyhow, I am now studying Biomedical Engineering, which wasn't a bad programme per se, but it isn't what I had been planning for for the past 3 years or so. I remember borderline hating this and hating myself for quite a while. But now that I have been studying for almost a month, I would say those have subsided a bit. Maybe this would be a good chance for me to get a high GPA, and eventually assist me in successfully transferring back into Medicine. Maybe this is the only way I could ever get into Medicine, and this is some sort of fated path? Honestly, I think I am both too optimistic and pessimistic at the same time. I mean that was literally the exact thought process I went through when the DSE appeal results were out. It's kinda like the old Chinese folklore tale about the old guy who lost the horse. Let's hope that this would be the case even though deep down in my heart I secretly believe that something would decide that it would like to go wrong. Murphy's Law I guess, basically concludes my life thus far. Yes, I may be less upset and more accepting of my current situation, but I kinda doubt it would stay this way for long. Maybe I should stop obsessing over choosing my own path, and just takes whatever life delivers, buddha style or something like that, but it really sounds like dubious advice to accept whatever. I guess you could only either live like whatever without a care in the world or keep dreaming about your aspirations and be disappointed constantly. I'm not sure I want to fall under either of those umbrellas but I don't suppose there is a third way, not an easy one at least.
>
> I still haven't taken a look at the DSE papers as of the writing of this. It's rather funny that my mentality would change so fast. The original reason I wanted the papers back was because I was in disbelief and wanted to get to the bottom of this. Again, in some sort of stages of grief. But now that I actually got them, I think maybe I have stopped caring about it. It would be poetic that I would just throw them away or store them somewhere forever, never to be opened. But I think I will eventually take a look at them, just not in the near future. Let's see in what mindset I would be in the next time I shall discover them among the piles of mess on my desk. This journal shall expect an entry about that by then, whenever it shall happen.

### 29-09-2021
#### Spoon-feedy education
> Don't expect much from this entry because I am writing this during a lecture, which is admittedly, not a very good thing to do. But in my defence, this lecture is quite dry. Not that it's boring though, the topic is interesting, but such spoon-feeding lectures prove to be an ineffective way to learn, for me at least. To some extent it may be good, but it really depends on the subject and topic. For instance, this particular lecture is about Anatomy and Physiology. With a load of information as big as this, it is practically impossible for anyone to comprehend when given in this style.
>
> The point of education is to shape the way we tackle new information, in my opinion. Mathematics actually demonstrates this idea perfectly; for every new theory that you discover, you'll *have* to understand all of its intricacies and limitations for it to provide you with the greatest asset. There should simply be no shortcuts. Spoon-feeding straight up kills it. Elegant theorems are often reduced into identities students have to recite if they possess even the slightest bit of sophistication. I'm confident to say that this is the major contributor to students getting confused and disinterested in Mathematics. It is tragic, yes, to know that the worst student could have been a great mathematician, if not for this system. Everything is optimised for speed, and anything that doesn't result in direct teaching progress shall be eliminated as soon as possible. Although it may be able to discourage teachers from straying from the topic, it also encourages them to speedrun through the materials. Comprehension, skipped; consolidation, skipped; the "click" moments, all skipped. After all, there is not a more direct and straightforward way to have them perform well in tests and exams than reciting. Do you not understand the material? Well too bad, for it is your job to keep up. Students simply don't have enough time to marinate themselves in the knowledge. They don't perform well in those subjects and grow to hate them. I can't blame them. It is only human to repulse from things that make your uncomfortable. Pardon me when I say that I don't have a solution for this, but this problem must be seen by the world. Are we sure that geniuses in this system would translate into geniuses of humanity?
>
> While what I've said is true for lower-level education, it is most certainly not true for tertiary ones (i.e. university education). Teachers, or rather, professors, have nothing more to be regulated by than the demands of society, the specialty required by the profession, and their own morals. After all, it's not like anyone would notice it unless it manifests itself in the society in the form of decreased competence. But professors are scholars, and most scholars are respectful to their own field and profession. It is merely their own moral obligation and social responsibility to educate as much and as thoroughly as their capabilities allow. Medicine is probably one of the, if not the only profession that leaves so little room for error. It is no joke to be a professional in this field. The spoon-feedy nature of this curriculum is simply the natural inevitability that such a system make arise. I guess students in this field are expected to be more competent and hence should be more than capable to "teach themselves". I've heard that students are expected to self-study for three times as much than actual lecture hours, and that's for other professions. I can only imagine how much that coefficient would be for medical students.
>
> I guess I'm half a medical student even right now, and I *am* expected to give and show that kind of effort and dedication toward my studies. Even though I currently only have to take one course this demanding, I'm already slipping big time. I can't imagine it would get any better than this, and it most likely won't. This course is seriously making me doubt my abilities to study Medicine in the event that I should find myself be successfully transferred to it. I guess I can try and wait and see.
#### Undiagnosed ailments
> I was feeling a little light-headed in today's PE lesson, except we haven't even started doing anything yet. We were just sitting in a small classroom listening to theories taught by the coach. Maybe it was because I had to turn my head slightly to the left to see him properly, maybe it was because I haven't drunk enough water yet in the morning. But mostly likely it should be because I have some undiagnosed disease. I can't for the life of me remember if this had been a thing for my entire life but it started manifest itself more and more during my senior secondary school years. I suspect I may have a wide variety of undiagnosed syndromes/disorders, but let's work our way up from the most inconsequential ones.
>
> For starters, I suspect that I may have ***ADHD (Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder)***. I could never sit in one place still without moving around, adjusting my pose every once in a while, fidgeting with my fingers or other objects, having restless legs... the list goes on. Even in this very moment, as of the writing of this, I can't help constantly shaking and moving. Basically, if I hold long enough in the same pose, be it standing or sitting, I would start to involuntarily enumerate all the slightest discomfort I'm feeling at the moment and *have* to do something to address them, which most commonly, turns into moving around for no reason. While this is nothing more than a nuisance at best for the large majority of the time, it can really draw attention to itself when there's nothing else to distract those around me from it.
>
> Next up: a slightly more annoying disorder that I suspect I may have sometimes. This is a strange one, because I vividly remember it popping up every once in a while in my childhood. It tended to stay for a while whenever it showed up, I'm talking a while as in a couple years; but on other days, I barely even remember it ever existing. It's like talking about it triggers it or something. ***OCD (Obsessive compulsory disorder)*** is the thing I'm talking about. It can manifest itself in numerous ways. From my experience, it can range from constantly blinking for no reason, to feeling the constant urge to make noises with my throat and nose, to twitching various muscle groups, to feeling the urge to touch the corners of objects, and to feeling the urge to bump my knees against each other. It is somewhat similar to ***TS (Tourette Syndrome)*** but to a milder and more voluntary(?) extent. I'm not even sure that OCD is the right term for it and it mostly likely isn't. TS should be more accurate but it's among the same field of disorders. Yes, it is weird, and yes, it bothers me quite a bit too because of its side effects like dry throat, sore muscles, and such. I believe it manifests when I'm under stress for extended periods of time. It kinda subsided for a long while but it can still pop up every now and then for a few days or so, until I forget about it and stop trying to stop it.
>
> ***Allergy***. My nose is allergic to god-knows-what, always. It and my lips are always dry. It would just swell and puff up and itch all the time. It gets worse with dry weather. I have to keep rubbing it. There is no other way of dealing with it. Trust me when I say I've tried. Lotions, medications, Chinese Medicine, nasal sprays. You name it, I've tried it. So far the most effective "treating" it is to apply a thin layer of Vaseline and really rub it in. But even it only acts as a temporary measure, effective for not long. Yes, I have to carry a small container of Vaseline with my all the time; so if you were wondering why my pockets are so full of random stuff, here's one for you. Admittedly, the situation has improved a lot as I've aged but it is just never quite gone. Nuisance, nuisance.
>
> I'm no stranger to ***GI (Gastrointestinal) problems***. I practically suffer from one of those every week or so. Basically anything can trigger stomachaches and make a very bad day. To be honest, they are so frequent I have more or less gotten used to them. They come and go as they please with no rhythm or reason. They would find any reason to start making a ruckus, sometimes even none at all. I'm also virtually 100% certain that I'm ***lactose intolerant***, so that's one genre of food I can stay the fuck away from. Honestly, I wish I could talk more about this but like I said I have gotten so used to them it might as well be part of my official weekly routine.
>
> Now that we've gone through all the minute and relatively insignificant bits, let's tread into the deep end. I believe I have some sort of ***anaemia***, not a severe case by any means, but it's bothersome and troubling. My mom and her family have a history of such a disease, so it's not surprising that I may have it. Anaemia, in essence, allows my blood to carry less oxygen around than normal. As such, I am really sensitive to oxygen level changes. Where others may not even notice, I can get slightly disoriented and light-headed when the oxygen level drops by any amount. Should the oxygen level drop for whatever reason, say, stale air due to a bad ventilation system, my brain starts to slow and I start to become less sensitive to my surroundings (i.e. my senses start to fade in a vague sense). Even in the shower, when there is too much steam, symptoms start to manifest. Like I said, I don't remember this ever being a problem in my childhood. I hypothesise that it manifesting only now is because I have grown and I have to use a larger portion of my cardiac capabilities to sustain myself, whereas when I was still small I could easily operate on half cardiac capacity. (Maybe this is the real reason why I'm restless - to pump blood around quicker? Not sure how scientific this deduction is though.) Over the years, I have learned to notice and identify the symptoms. It is not as easy as it sounds. Noticing yourself being delusional is a really tough feat, especially when the oxygen level drops so quickly the symptoms kick in right away instead of gradually. But let's get back to the point. Decreased cardiac effectiveness holds me back from a lot of stuff. I can't do medium-to-intense exercises without approaching the verge of a blackout and I can't recover at a normal pace. The most annoying thing is that I get tired all the god-damn time. I never seem to be able to get through a single day without feeling tired half-way. Perhaps this is the real reason I procrastinate and never managed to finish everything I wanted to finish within specific timeframes. I want to do so much yet I *can* do so little. I'm basically always on half battery despite only waking up 2-3 hours prior. I'm not sure there could be a solution/alleviation to this aliment, but I'm starting to take on more exercises as required by the university Physical Education, so let's see how that would go.
>
> Did you think anaemia was the biggest thing? Far from it. I suspect I may also have some type of ***diabetes***. Thinking back, I believe the earliest sign was from my primary school years. I would always get thirsty when I'm trying to sleep. I would get up every minute or so to have sips of water, then I'll have to use the washroom because of all the water, and the cycle continues. It would take a really long time for me to fall asleep because of this. I basically can only rest when this whole ordeal tires me out to the point where I forget that I'm thirsty. Fast forward to today, I still drink water more frequently than those around me. It's like I'm doing transpiration all the time or something like that. Even when I'm just sitting still doing nothing I could still feel thirsty. And on bad days I could chug a whole bottle of water and it would do nothing to quench my thirst. I am always feeling dry. Washroom is a very popular venue in my daily routine. All this fuss about hydration and here I am suffering from basically the opposite problem. Now that I think about it, this could also go hand-in-hand with my anaemia to make me very prone to light-headedness, 'cus it also make my blood sugar level fluctuate quite a bit; could also be another reason why I'm always feeling tired.
>
> I'm well aware I am weak and frail like some 80-year-old. I'm already looking for a full-body check and hopefully I can get one soon. Let's see how accurate my self-diagnosis is, although I'm not sure I'd like to be proven correct.

### 02-10-2021
#### The undocumented and sealed-away memories
> Yes, I am aware that I have skipped making an entry yesterday. There was no particular reason as to why, only that I didn't do it. Granted, it was a holiday, so my sleep schedule was non-existent. To be fair, I stayed up all night after only having a small nap yesterday, so I'm kinda still living in the yesterday but that's besides the point. I was feeling anxious yesterday. Something was on my mind. I know for certain what the thing that had been clouding me is, but I do not wish to admit it, or even acknowledge it.
> 
> That brings me to my point, that not everything should be documented. I know I have mentioned when I started this journey that every day should have something worthy of a journal entry, that something as insignificant as a random thought I had during the day could suffice. Actually, Toby and I have briefly talked about this when he was convincing me to start this. But some things are just best left in the past. Some things are not meant to be remembered. If I wish something to not be in my life, not to be a factor in the definition of my character and my future decision-making, then it shall not be remembered. And I believe this should go for everyone. If a thought poses discomfort in you, but has little significance to anyone else, you should simply discard it - but don't try too hard, mind you, lest it become one of those unforgettable forgotten memories that would loom over you for the rest of your life. You should know what I'm talking about: repressed memories, denial, whatever you want to call it, they are the thoughts/memories you try your hardest to eliminate from existence yet inevitably re-emerges to haunt you from time to time. I have specifically not put the "thing"-in-question here, as you might have noticed. Generally, I've found that the best way to deal with them is to exploit your psychology. Try thinking about the same scenario, but in some sort of alternative timeline, where you had actually done or not done the thing that's troubling you, or something you would've done differently. Think of multiple of them. And here's the thing: try to be as vivid as possible. As unintuitive and counter-productive this may sound, it can actually help with overshadowing the actual event itself, until the point where you either cease to remember the event, or are no longer able to tell the real event from the crowd of alternative universes you have formulated. You see, your brain loves to latch onto bad memories. It's a pretty reasonable survival adaptation if you think about it. As such, negative events are significantly more likely to be remembered and subsequently recalled than positive ones. By mixing such an event with its positive alternatives, it could trick the brain into treating it as though it *is* a positive event, and have it be forgotten sooner. Making the false "universes" vivid is important because, our brains hate to have memories be vague and would always prefer to keep the more vivid version, for the same reason as before. I did not intend to make this some sort of science/psychology argument, but science and its sort interest me intrinsically. I have found varying degrees of success with this method. Some of them became vague and foggy, and maybe I've already forgotten some of them, which I obviously am not able to elaborate on.
>
> Is this some sort of lying? Definitely. But I would argue not all kinds of lying is negative. *"We will always be the best judge of our actions, and they will always be punished in their own respect. A small crime will make you guilty for a short while, and a big one will make you feel awful for a very very long time, perhaps even for the entirety of the rest of your life."* That was a poorly-done paraphrase of a thing I've heard somewhere before, and I agree with it. Guilt is the best punishment against crimes, the same way looming mental worms are for missteps and misdecisions. This should be evident enough that we shouldn't try to evade them, until we consider that our brains also love to exaggerate situations and blow things out of proportions. Sometimes they can even be not in our control and we'll still have to suffer the consequences of it in the form of mental anguish. I am most definitely going out on a limb here, but I believe this could even be some sort of treatment for more, actually traumatic events, or at least be able to alleviate them. This method would be some sort of appeal system for our brains' auto-judgement I suppose. I feel like the analogy is starting to break down, but I hope I got the point out in time before this all melt into a pot of meaningless ideas and incomprehensible brain vomit. Every seal eventually leaks; and the only way to prevent that, is to have nothing to be sealed in the first place.
